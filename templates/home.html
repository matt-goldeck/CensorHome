The Network Science Lab at MSU

About

The Network Science Lab (NetSci) in the Department of Computer Science at Montclair State University is dedicated to the study of the intersection between social behavior and computational systems. NetSci supports a broad range of topics including: Internet censorship, Internet of Things (IoT), security and privacy, and the modeling and analysis of technological and online social networks. Researchers in lab have expertise in network analytic methods, machine learning, and natural language processing.

An experimental test-bed enables exploration and simulation activities that support the underlying research conducted in the lab. Overview of the network topology that supports our research.

Projects

Internet Censorship Detection

Detecting Censored Content

Internet regulation in the form of online censorship and Internet shutdowns have been increasing over recent years. This project involves the development of a natural language processing (NLP) application for performing cross country probing that conceals the exact location of the originating request.

Classifiying Controversial Content

This project investigates the performance of multi-class classifiers using novel linguistic features in order to determine the most useful classifier for detecting controversial news articles.

Linguistic Fingerprinting

This project investigates linguistic characteristics of censored and uncensored posts on a number of topics to build a classifier that predicts censorship decisions independent of discussion topics.

Internet Shutdowns

The objective of this research is to provide a methodology for measuring the financial impacts of Internet outages. The financial impacts are measured against a Nation’s Gross Domestic Product (GDP) for countries to project the aftermath of Internet outage episodes.

IoT

Remote Radio Frequency Analysis and Jamming on the Internet of Things

This project investigates radio frequency (RF) jamming, selective RF (SRF) jamming, and undetectable selective RF (USRF) jamming in a step-by-step approach using several categories of RF devices, starting with simple RF doorbells. Increasingly sophisticated devices will be tested and documented, with the ultimate goal of producing an easy to use tool consisting of hardware and software to be used for testing the security and resilience of Internet of Things IoT devices.

PiMesh – Implementation of Mesh Network on Multiple Raspberry Pi’s

This work explores optimal routing strategies for wireless mesh networks using a series of Raspberry Pi’s. implementing a wireless mesh network on multiple Raspberry Pi’s. Topics investigated in this research include fundamental challenges with the mesh network such as battery power, latency and redundancy.

Team

Faculty

Christopher Leberknight

Associate Professor

Chris is an Associate Professor of Computer Science at Montclair State University and Director of the Network Science Lab (NETSCI). His primary field of research is network science which focuses on the modeling and analysis of networks from the technological (network and algorithmic topics), ecological, and social perspectives. His research has been published in top tier journals as well as national and international conferences. In addition to his academic experience Chris has over 10 years of professional experience in the computer and telecommunication industries and served as the CEO for two start-up companies. He also has experience with decision support and location aware systems (patent # 7,406,448). Chris is a member of the IEEE, Association of Computing Machinery, and the Association for Information Systems and serves on the editorial board for the Journal of Privacy and Information Security, International Journal of Internet of Things and Cyber-Assurance, and the International Journal of E-Politics.

Anna Feldman

Professor

Anna is a computational linguist. She has worked on a number of different projects, such as cross-lingual approaches to morpho-syntactic tagging, learner corpora, and automatic idiom recognition. She is currently a Co-PI (with Chris Leberknight) on the project that applies linguistic insights to measure and circumvent Internet censorship. She co-authored a book on resource-light morphology, co-edited nine volumes on various topics related to NLP and published 40+ peer-reviewed articles in journals and conference proceedings. Her work has been funded by the US Army Research Lab, the US Air Force Research Lab, the US National Science Foundation.
